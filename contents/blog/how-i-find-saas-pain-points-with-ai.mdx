Most indie hackers build what they wish existed. It's the "scratch your own itch" wisdom that's launched a thousand Products of the Day. The problem? Your itches are often idiosyncratic. Your pains might be rare. The market might not care.

I run a different playbook. Instead of building what I wish existed, I point AI at the feedback firehose — Reddit threads, G2 reviews, App Store complaints, Hacker News "Ask HN: why does X still suck?" posts — and let it surface what people are *actually* screaming about.

## Why feedback data beats surveys

Surveys are answered by people who agreed to be surveyed. Review sites and community forums are answered by people with strong enough opinions to seek out a place to express them. That selection bias is actually *good* for our purposes: high-intensity pain correlates strongly with willingness to pay to make that pain go away.

Here's what the data sources look like in practice:

**Reddit** — `/r/[productname]` threads are a goldmine. The "why does this still not do X" posts are product management gold. `/r/entrepreneur`, `/r/SaaS`, and category-specific subreddits surface cross-product pain.

**G2 and Capterra** — structured reviews with star breakdowns. The 2-star and 3-star reviews are the most useful: specific enough to be actionable, not ragequit-unhinged like 1-stars often are.

**App Store reviews** — especially useful for mobile-first tools. Filter to 2-3 stars, recent, and watch the patterns.

**Hacker News** — "Ask HN: What tool do you use for X and why do you hate it?" threads are genuinely invaluable.

## The n8n automation

The Pain Point Radar widget above calls an n8n webhook that runs the following pipeline:

1. **Reddit scrape** — pulls the top 50 posts containing your market term from relevant subreddits, filtered by upvotes and recency
2. **Review site fetch** — hits G2's RSS feeds and Capterra's public review data for competing products
3. **Sentiment + clustering** — Claude (Haiku, for cost) clusters the complaints into themes and ranks them by frequency × intensity
4. **Opportunity extraction** — a second pass asks Claude to identify the *gap* most frequently adjacent to the top pain points

The entire pipeline runs in under 90 seconds for most markets. The n8n workflow uses HTTP Request nodes, a Code node for deduplication, and two AI Agent nodes — one for clustering, one for the opportunity synthesis.

## Reading the output

The intensity score you see in the widget is a composite: frequency of the pain mentioned (normalized across sources) × average sentiment polarity × a recency weight that decays results older than six months.

Scores above 75 mean the pain is both widespread and acutely felt — these are the markets worth building in. Scores below 40 mean complaints exist but the intensity doesn't suggest a buy decision.

The "top pain points" are verbatim-adjacent — they're lightly paraphrased clusters of the most common specific complaints. Pay attention to the *specificity*: "the onboarding is confusing" is low-signal; "the API key is buried three levels deep in Settings > Advanced > Developer, and there's no documentation for the webhook format" is high-signal.

## Building the validation moat

Once you've found a high-intensity pain, the AI-assisted research workflow doesn't stop. I run a second pass to answer:

- **How many people pay to solve adjacent problems?** (proxy: competitor Crunchbase funding, number of G2 reviews = rough market size signal)
- **What have people already tried?** (look for "I switched from X" posts — they tell you what didn't work)
- **Is this a hair-on-fire problem or a vitamin?** (hair-on-fire: people have hacked together workarounds, which means they've already demonstrated willingness to invest time)

If all three signals are green, you're looking at a market worth a landing page and a 10-conversation validation sprint.

## The actual workflow, step by step

Here's how I run this in practice:

**Week 1** — Run the Pain Point Radar on 5 markets I'm curious about. Pick the top 2 by intensity score.

**Week 2** — Deep-dive the top 2 with manual review reading. Confirm the AI summary matches what I see when I read 30-40 reviews myself. (It usually does, with occasional hallucination on specifics — always spot-check.)

**Week 3** — Build a landing page describing the solution to the #1 pain point. No product, just the promise. Run $100 in traffic to it. Watch bounce rate and email capture rate.

**Week 4** — If the landing page converts, DM or email 10 people who mentioned the pain in public forums. Offer a 20-minute call. If 3+ agree, you have a real problem to solve.

This takes four weeks and costs less than $200. The alternative — build for six months and then validate — costs six months.

## Where this breaks down

The AI-assisted approach has real blind spots:

**Enterprise pain is invisible here.** Enterprise buyers don't post on Reddit. They might leave a G2 review, but they're under NDA about specifics. If you're going upmarket, this method gives you a floor, not a ceiling.

**Niche professional pain is underrepresented.** A real estate attorney's workflow pain doesn't show up in Reddit's top posts. Niche pain requires niche communities — Discord servers, LinkedIn groups, professional forums.

**The vocal minority problem.** Power users who know how to find Reddit aren't the median user. A pain screamed about loudly on forums might be felt by only 5% of the user base.

For these cases, I supplement the AI radar with direct cold outreach to practitioners — but that's a separate post.
